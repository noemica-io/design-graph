The work happens before the picking.

A short argument against the quiet, expensive habit of deciding what to make before you have seen what you could have made.

Designers are trained to converge. To narrow. To pick a direction in the first hour of the first meeting and then spend the next three weeks defending it in increasingly elaborate ways. The craft, we are told, is in the defending. The taste is in the picking.

This is mostly wrong. The defending is theater. The picking, done early, is a guess dressed up as a decision. The real work — the part where an idea gets better — happens in the space of the options you did not take. You can only see what a design is by standing it next to what it almost was.

The tax we pay for single-path design is not the variants we skipped. It is the quiet certainty that we shipped the right one, because we never let the others exist long enough to disagree with us.
